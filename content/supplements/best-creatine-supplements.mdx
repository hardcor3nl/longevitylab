## Why Creatine Is the Most Important Longevity Supplement You're Not Taking Seriously

Creatine has a reputation problem. Most people associate it with bodybuilders and protein shakes. This is a mistake.

Creatine is one of the most studied compounds in the history of nutritional science — over 500 randomised controlled trials — and its benefits extend far beyond muscle size. For longevity specifically, creatine addresses two of the most significant age-related declines: **sarcopenia** (muscle loss) and **cognitive deterioration**.

If you are over 35 and not taking creatine, you are leaving one of the most evidence-backed interventions on the table.

---

## How Creatine Works

Creatine is stored in muscle and brain tissue as phosphocreatine. It functions as a rapid ATP resynthesis system — your cells' fastest energy currency.

During high-intensity effort (lifting, sprinting, intense cognitive work), ATP depletes within seconds. Phosphocreatine donates its phosphate group to replenish ATP immediately, extending high-intensity output before fatigue sets in.

**Beyond acute energy:** Creatine supplementation increases total creatine stores by 20–40%, which:
- Accelerates post-exercise recovery
- Reduces muscle protein breakdown
- Supports mitochondrial function
- Improves glucose uptake into muscle

---

## The Longevity Case for Creatine

### 1. Sarcopenia Prevention

After age 30, we lose 3–8% of muscle mass per decade. After 60, the rate accelerates. Sarcopenia (muscle wasting) is one of the strongest independent predictors of all-cause mortality, fall risk, hospitalisation, and loss of independence.

Creatine supplementation combined with resistance training produces **significantly greater lean mass gains** than resistance training alone across multiple meta-analyses. In older adults specifically, the effect size is meaningful: a 2017 meta-analysis of 22 RCTs found creatine + resistance training increased lean mass by an additional 1.37 kg versus training alone.

### 2. Cognitive Function

The brain is the second largest consumer of creatine in the body. Cerebral creatine declines with age, and this decline correlates with cognitive deterioration.

**Key findings:**
- Creatine supplementation improves working memory and processing speed in vegetarians and vegans (who have lower baseline levels)
- Single dose of 20g improved intelligence test performance under sleep deprivation
- Ongoing research suggests protective effects against neurodegeneration — preliminary but mechanistically plausible

### 3. Bone Density

Emerging research suggests creatine may support bone mineral density — particularly relevant for postmenopausal women at risk of osteoporosis. A 2014 RCT found women taking creatine during resistance training lost significantly less bone mineral content at the femoral neck than placebo.

## Creatine Monohydrate vs. Other Forms

The supplement industry has created dozens of creatine "variations" — creatine HCl, buffered creatine (Kre-Alkalyn), creatine ethyl ester, creatine nitrate. Most are more expensive and less studied.

**The evidence is unambiguous:** creatine monohydrate achieves equivalent or superior muscle saturation at lower cost. The "better absorption" claims of premium forms are not supported by direct comparison studies.

| Form | Absorption | Evidence | Cost |
|------|-----------|----------|------|
| Creatine Monohydrate | High | 500+ RCTs | Low |
| Creatine HCl | Similar | ~10 studies | 3–5x higher |
| Kre-Alkalyn | Similar | ~5 studies | 3x higher |
| Creatine Ethyl Ester | Lower | Few studies | Higher |

**Verdict: Buy creatine monohydrate.** The only reason to consider other forms is gastrointestinal sensitivity with monohydrate — in which case micronized monohydrate (smaller particle size) typically resolves the issue.

## Dosing Protocol

**Standard protocol (no loading):**
- 3–5g daily with any meal
- Consistent daily use — creatine works through saturation, not acute dosing
- No need to cycle off

**Loading protocol (faster saturation, optional):**
- 20g/day split into 4 doses for 5–7 days
- Then 3–5g maintenance daily
- Useful if you want faster results, but not necessary

**Timing:** Timing is largely irrelevant. Post-workout may offer a marginal benefit but the effect is small. The most important variable is consistency.

**With carbs:** Taking creatine with carbohydrates slightly improves uptake via insulin-mediated transport. Adding it to a post-workout shake with fruit or mixing with juice is a simple optimization.

## Who Benefits Most

- **Adults 35+** prioritising muscle preservation
- **Vegetarians and vegans** (significantly lower dietary creatine intake)
- **Older adults 60+** with sarcopenia risk
- **Cognitive workers** who want the brain-energy benefits
- **Athletes** in any sport requiring explosive or repeated effort

## Side Effects and Safety

Creatine is one of the safest supplements studied. Common concerns addressed:

**Kidney damage:** Extensively studied — no evidence of harm in healthy individuals. The concern stems from creatine's conversion to creatinine, a kidney marker — but this is dietary, not pathological elevation.

**Hair loss:** A 2009 study in rugby players found DHT (a testosterone metabolite linked to hair loss) increased 56% after loading. This is a single study, not replicated. If you have pattern baldness concerns, the risk is theoretical but worth being aware of.

**Water retention:** Creatine draws water into muscle cells — this is part of the mechanism. Scale weight may increase 1–2kg initially. Body fat does not increase.
