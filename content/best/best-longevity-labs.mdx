## Why Standard Blood Tests Fail You

A standard blood panel ordered by a GP or family physician typically includes:

- Complete blood count (CBC)
- Basic metabolic panel (sodium, potassium, creatinine, glucose)
- Standard lipid panel (total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides)
- TSH (thyroid stimulating hormone)

This is useful for detecting gross abnormality. It is not designed for longevity medicine. The problem is not what it measures — it is what it misses.

**What standard labs miss:**

- **ApoB** — the best predictor of cardiovascular disease risk (better than LDL-C)
- **Lp(a)** — a genetic risk factor for cardiovascular disease affecting ~20% of people, undetectable on a standard lipid panel
- **Fasting insulin** — the earliest marker of insulin resistance; not included in standard metabolic panels
- **hsCRP** — high-sensitivity CRP for low-grade inflammation; standard CRP misses subclinical inflammation
- **Homocysteine** — elevated in B vitamin deficiency; associated with cardiovascular and cognitive risk
- **Full thyroid panel** (free T3, free T4, reverse T3) — standard TSH alone misses peripheral conversion problems
- **Sex hormones** (free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol, DHEA-S) — not ordered without specific complaint
- **IGF-1** — growth hormone surrogate; not routine
- **Vitamin D** (25-OH) — often tested but frequently not acted on appropriately

A comprehensive longevity panel costs $200–500 self-pay. The same tests ordered through insurance billing can cost $3,000–5,000 — if the physician will even order them.

### Ulta Lab Tests / Walk-In Lab

**Best for:** Ordering specific tests you already know you want, at the lowest possible cost

These are direct-to-consumer lab ordering platforms — you choose the test, pay online, get a requisition, walk into a Quest Diagnostics or LabCorp draw site, and receive results directly.

**Advantages:**
- Lowest cost for individual tests (ApoB: ~$30, Lp(a): ~$35, fasting insulin: ~$28)

**Disadvantages:**

## The Core Longevity Panel: What to Order

Regardless of which lab you use, these are the highest-priority tests for a comprehensive longevity baseline:

### Cardiovascular
- **ApoB** — the most important single cardiovascular risk marker; primary target of statin therapy
- **Lp(a)** — genetic; test once; if elevated (&gt;100 nmol/L or &gt;50 mg/dL), monitor closely
- **hsCRP** — low-grade inflammation; target &lt;1.0 mg/L
- **Homocysteine** — target &lt;10 µmol/L; responds to B vitamins
- **Triglycerides** — target &lt;1.0 mmol/L (150 mg/dL); best marker of carbohydrate and alcohol excess

### Metabolic
- **Fasting insulin** — target &lt;8 µIU/mL (some longevity physicians target &lt;5)
- **HOMA-IR** (calculated) — target &lt;1.5
- **HbA1c** — target 4.8–5.3%; below 5.7% is generally considered normal but longevity-focused range is tighter
- **Uric acid** — target &lt;5.5 mg/dL; elevated uric acid associated with metabolic and cardiovascular risk

### Hormonal
- **Free and total testosterone** — relevant for both men and women for muscle, mood, and metabolic health
- **SHBG** — interprets free fraction of testosterone
- **DHEA-S** — adrenal androgen; declines with age; marker of adrenal reserve
- **IGF-1** — growth hormone proxy; neither too high nor too low is ideal
- **Full thyroid** (TSH + free T3 + free T4 + reverse T3) — TSH alone misses most functional thyroid issues

### Inflammation and Nutrition
- **Ferritin** — iron storage; elevated ferritin is a cardiovascular and inflammatory risk factor independent of anaemia
- **25-OH Vitamin D** — target 40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L)
- **B12 and folate** — especially if taking metformin (B12 depletion) or following a plant-based diet

## Testing Frequency

**First test:** Comprehensive baseline — all markers above

**Annual:** Full panel to track trends year-over-year

**Quarterly:** Core 6–8 markers if actively optimising (ApoB, fasting insulin, hsCRP, vitamin D, testosterone, HbA1c)

**After dietary or supplement changes:** Retest relevant markers 8–12 weeks after meaningful protocol changes to assess impact

The trend over time is more informative than any single test. Tracking ApoB across 5 years tells a more useful story than knowing your ApoB today without context.
