## What Is HRV?

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) is the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. Despite what the name suggests, a higher HRV is healthier — meaning your heart does *not* beat like a metronome, but instead shows beat-to-beat variation.

This variation is controlled by the autonomic nervous system (ANS) — the system that governs automatic bodily functions. The ANS has two branches:

- **Sympathetic nervous system** ("fight or flight"): increases heart rate, reduces variability
- **Parasympathetic nervous system** ("rest and digest"): decreases heart rate, increases variability

High HRV indicates strong parasympathetic tone — your body is in a state of recovery, restoration, and readiness. Low HRV indicates sympathetic dominance — your body is under stress, fighting infection, under-recovered, or physiologically strained.

HRV is essentially a real-time window into your body's stress-recovery balance.

---

## Why HRV Predicts Longevity

The autonomic nervous system controls cardiac rhythm, blood pressure regulation, digestion, immune function, and inflammation. Chronic sympathetic dominance (low HRV) accelerates every one of these systems toward dysfunction.

**Associations with low HRV:**
- Increased all-cause mortality (strongest in cardiovascular disease)
- Sudden cardiac death risk
- Depression and anxiety
- Insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome
- Chronic inflammation
- Cognitive decline

**High HRV is associated with:**
- Elite athletic performance
- Better stress management
- Faster recovery from illness and exercise
- Improved cognitive flexibility
- Longer healthspan

A landmark 2015 study following 2,763 adults over 14 years found that each 10ms increase in RMSSD (the standard HRV metric) was associated with a 20% reduction in cardiovascular mortality.

## How HRV Is Measured

### The Metric: RMSSD
The most used HRV metric is **RMSSD** (Root Mean Square of Successive Differences) — a measure of short-term variability reflecting parasympathetic activity. This is what WHOOP, Oura, Garmin, and most consumer wearables report.

RMSSD is typically measured in milliseconds (ms).

### Gold Standard: ECG
A medical-grade ECG chest strap (Polar H10 is the consumer gold standard) during a 5-minute resting measurement delivers the most accurate HRV data. Used by researchers and serious athletes.

### Consumer Wearables
- **WHOOP 4.0:** Measures during sleep onset — best wrist-based accuracy
- **Oura Ring Gen 3:** Ring-based PPG, measured during sleep — strong accuracy
- **Garmin (HRV Status):** Measured during sleep, 7-day rolling average
- **Apple Watch:** On-demand 1-minute spot check — less accurate than sleep measurement

All consumer devices have measurement error of 5–20ms compared to ECG. Use them for trends, not absolute values.

### Measurement Timing
HRV is most stable and reproducible when measured:
- During deep sleep (most devices)
- Immediately upon waking before getting out of bed (5-minute lying measurement)
- Not during or immediately after exercise, caffeine, stress, or meals

## What Are "Normal" HRV Values?

HRV is **highly individual** and declines significantly with age. Comparing your number to someone else's is largely meaningless — what matters is your trend relative to your own baseline.

**Population averages by age (RMSSD, ms):**

- **20–25 years:** 55–105 ms
- **25–35 years:** 45–90 ms
- **35–45 years:** 35–70 ms
- **45–55 years:** 25–55 ms
- **55–65 years:** 20–45 ms
- **65+:** 15–35 ms

Elite endurance athletes can have values 50–100% above age norms. Long-term meditators show similarly elevated values.

**The key principle:** Your goal is to be in the top 25% for your age group and to trend upward over months to years.

## What Lowers HRV (The Suppressors)

Understanding what tanks HRV gives you direct leverage over it:

**Immediate suppressors (24–48h effect):**
- Alcohol (most potent acute suppressor — even 1–2 drinks measurably reduces next-day HRV)
- Poor sleep quality or insufficient duration
- High training load without adequate recovery
- Illness or infection (HRV drops 1–3 days before subjective symptoms)
- Psychological stress and anxiety
- Processed food and high sugar intake

**Chronic suppressors:**
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Chronic sleep deprivation
- Obesity and metabolic syndrome
- Chronic psychological stress
- Social isolation

---

## What Raises HRV (The Elevators)

**Highest impact (weeks to months):**
- Regular aerobic exercise — particularly Zone 2 training (most potent long-term HRV elevator)
- Strength training
- Consistent sleep (both duration and timing)
- Meditation and mindfulness practice (even 10 minutes daily shows measurable effect within 8 weeks)

**Moderate impact:**
- Cold exposure (immediate parasympathetic activation)
- Slow, controlled breathing (4–6 breaths/minute — the most powerful acute HRV elevator)
- Reducing alcohol
- Improving nutrition quality

**Acute elevators (same-day effect):**
- 5 minutes of slow breathing at 0.1 Hz (6 breaths/minute)
- Short meditation session
- Cold shower or plunge
- Nap (if sleep-deprived)

## Coherent Breathing: The Most Powerful Acute HRV Protocol

Breathing at approximately 6 breaths per minute (5 seconds in, 5 seconds out) creates resonance with the heart rate oscillation frequency — dramatically amplifying HRV in the moment and building parasympathetic tone over time.

**Protocol:**
1. Lie or sit comfortably
2. Inhale for 5 seconds (through the nose, into the belly)
3. Exhale for 5 seconds (slow, controlled)
4. Continue for 5 minutes minimum; 20 minutes for training effect

Practised daily for 8 weeks, this protocol significantly increases baseline HRV and reduces anxiety and blood pressure. It is the basis of biofeedback therapy and the Wim Hof Method structure.

Apps: **Elite HRV**, **HRV4Training**, and **Morpheus** can guide coherent breathing with real-time HRV display.

## How to Use HRV Practically

### Do Not Chase the Number Daily
HRV fluctuates naturally by 10–20ms day-to-day due to normal physiological variation. Reacting to a single low reading creates anxiety that further suppresses HRV. Look at 7-day rolling averages.

### Use the Trend
A week of consistently low HRV relative to your 30-day baseline is meaningful signal. It typically means: reduce training intensity, prioritise sleep, check for illness onset, manage stress.

### Use It for Training Decisions
- **HRV above baseline:** Green light for hard training
- **HRV at baseline:** Moderate training appropriate
- **HRV below baseline:** Prioritise recovery — active rest, Zone 1 only
- **HRV significantly suppressed (10%+ below baseline):** Full rest, investigate cause

This is WHOOP's core use case — and the reason professional athletes use it religiously.

### Track Inputs Alongside HRV
The most useful HRV practice is logging your inputs (sleep hours, alcohol, training load, stress events, illness) alongside your HRV output. Within a few months, you will identify which specific inputs most affect your personal HRV — and this is more valuable than any population-level advice.
